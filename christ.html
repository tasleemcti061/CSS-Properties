CHRIST THE REDEEMER

Christ The Redeemer one of the 7 Wonders of the World


Today, Christ the Redeemer is one of the seven new wonders of the world, a title recently given to 7 world monuments to reflect the list of 7 ancient wonders. It is also the most famous monument in Brazil, and its symbole, such as the Statue of Liberty is for the United States, the Eiffel Tower for France, or the Taj Mahal for India.


Christ the Redeemer was built using reinforced concrete and has an outer shell of 6 million soapstone tiles. It's believed that the workers who made these tiles occasionally wrote notes on the back, meaning this iconic landmark is full of hidden messages.


Christ the Redeemer is made of concrete and covered in soapstone tiles. The material for the statue was transported up Mount Corcovado which is approximately 2,310 feet above sea level. Once the statue was completed, it was 98 feet (30 meters) in height. When measuring across from Christ the Redeemer's outstretched arms, it has a width of 92 feet (28 meters).


Christ the Redeemer was not actually built in Brazil. It was first made in France by French sculptor Paul Landowski who created the statue in clay pieces. These were then shipped to Brazil to be remade with reinforced concrete by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa and French engineer Albert Caquot.


Christ the Redeemer gazes from its perch on Mount Corcovado over the city of Rio de Janeiro in the South American country of Brazil. Work began on the colossal Art Deco Style statue in 1922. Art Deco was a popular movement in the 1920s characterized by geometric and curvilinear designs and tended to be linear and smooth in nature.
